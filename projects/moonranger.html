Context

MoonRanger is a small, fast lunar rover being built at Carnegie Mellon to demonstrate autonomous navigation on the moon — without continuous earth-based teleoperation. The mission targets prospecting for water ice near the lunar south pole.

My contribution

I worked on the stereo reconstruction pipeline, specifically adding functionality and unit tests for writing disparity map image data to disk for offline inspection and debugging.

The motivation is that stereo failures are hard to debug live. A disparity map can look plausible on a screen and still be quietly wrong — bad matches in low-texture regions, edge fattening, holes where the lunar surface has too little contrast. Being able to dump the raw disparity data to disk meant the perception team could replay a run frame by frame against ground truth and actually see where the pipeline broke, instead of guessing from a downstream navigation failure.

What I learned

This was my first time inside a research codebase that was actually headed for flight hardware. The bar for testing and reproducibility is different when the system has to run autonomously on the moon with no one able to patch it — every capability needs a way to be inspected and verified offline, and "it works on my machine" isn't a finish line.
