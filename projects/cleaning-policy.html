Contact-rich cleaning with learned policies.

An open-source ROS2 framework for contact-rich surface cleaning manipulation — combining diffusion policy with classical state machines, force feedback, and recovery behaviors. Built on the SO-101 arm from my XLERobot setup.

Motivation

Contact-rich manipulation — wiping, scrubbing, polishing — is exactly where pure learned policies struggle and pure classical control is too brittle. The interesting question isn't "which is better?" but "what's the right architecture for combining them?"

I worked on a real cleaning manipulator at Peanut Robotics for two years using almost entirely classical methods. This project is the inverse experiment: start from learned policies, fall back to classical control where it earns its place, and find the principled boundary between the two.

Approach

High-level architecture I'm exploring:

  • Diffusion policy for the contact-rich core motion — the part that benefits from imitation learning across many demonstrations.
  • Classical state machine for high-level task orchestration: approach, engage, clean, retract, recover.
  • Force feedback in the inner loop to bound contact forces regardless of what the policy outputs.
  • Recovery behaviors as classical fallbacks when the policy's predicted action falls outside a confidence envelope or when force/torque sensing detects an off-nominal interaction.

Current status

Collecting teleoperated demonstrations on the SO-101, standing up a baseline diffusion policy training pipeline through LeRobot, and scaffolding the ROS2 framework that will wrap the policy with the classical safety and recovery layers.

What I'm hoping to learn

  • How small a demonstration dataset can produce a useful contact-rich policy
  • Where the principled boundary between learned and classical lives
  • What recovery looks like for a learned policy that has no built-in concept of "I don't know"
  • How to expose enough hooks in the framework that others can plug in their own policies and fallback behaviors
